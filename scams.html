Scams

Common tricks scammers use — and how to protect yourself.

Quick Rules That Stop Most Scams

  • Never share one-time codes (text/email verification codes) with anyone.
  • Don’t click urgent links in emails/texts—go to the site/app manually instead.
  • Don’t trust caller ID. Scammers can fake phone numbers.
  • Pause and verify. Call the company back using a number from their official website.
  • Never pay with gift cards, crypto, or wire transfer for “support,” “fees,” or “refunds.”

If something feels rushed, threatening, or “too good to be true,” slow down. Scams depend on panic and speed.

Common Scams & Techniques

  • Phishing emails — messages pretending to be from banks, delivery services, or password alerts. They push you to click a link and sign in.
  • Smishing (text phishing) — texts like “package delivery failed” or “your account is locked” with a suspicious link.
  • Vishing (phone scams) — calls claiming to be your bank, Microsoft, Amazon, IRS, etc. They try to get personal info or payment.
  • Fake tech support popups — scary browser popups saying you have a virus and must call a number. Real security alerts do not demand phone calls.
  • Remote access scams — they convince you to install remote tools and then “show problems” or steal passwords/files.
  • Password reset / MFA code theft — they ask for your login code “to verify it’s you.” That code lets them take over your account.
  • Refund scams — they claim you’re owed a refund, then “accidentally” send too much and ask you to send money back (or they use fake bank screens).
  • Overpayment scams — common with selling items online. They “pay extra,” then ask you to refund the difference. Their original payment later bounces.
  • Romance scams — someone builds trust over time and then asks for money, gift cards, or crypto.
  • Job/remote work scams — fake hiring, fake checks, or asking you to buy equipment and “get reimbursed.”
  • Investment/crypto scams — guaranteed returns, “insider” tips, or fake trading platforms.
  • Impersonation scams — pretending to be your friend/family member who “lost their phone” and needs money fast.

Red Flags (If You See These, Stop)

  • Pressure: “Act now,” “last chance,” “you’ll be arrested,” “your account will be closed today.”
  • Payment methods: gift cards, crypto, wire transfer, or “pay by QR code.”
  • Requests for: passwords, one-time codes, or remote access.
  • Links that look “almost right” (misspellings, extra words, strange domains).
  • Sender addresses that don’t match the company (even if the email looks official).

What To Do If You Think You’re Being Scammed

  1. Stop communicating. Don’t click more links or continue the call/chat.
  2. Disconnect remote access immediately (turn off Wi-Fi / unplug ethernet) if someone got in.
  3. Change passwords (starting with email), and enable multi-factor authentication where possible.
  4. Check your accounts (bank, credit card, email, social) for unusual activity.
  5. Contact your bank/card provider if money was sent or card details were shared.
  6. Run a security scan and remove suspicious apps/extensions.

If you’re not sure what happened, it’s okay to ask for help. The goal is to limit damage quickly.

Safer Habits (Simple Prevention)

  • Use a password manager and unique passwords for important accounts.
  • Turn on MFA (authenticator app is better than SMS when available).
  • Keep your device and browser updated.
  • Install apps only from official stores and trusted vendors.
  • Be cautious with browser extensions — fewer is safer.
